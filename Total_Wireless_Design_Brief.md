# Design Brief: Total Wireless Conversational Experience Prototype

**Client:** Verizon / Total Wireless
**Timeline:** 2 hours (rapid prototype)
**Type:** Proof-of-concept / Visual Demo

---

## Overview

Transform the existing Total Wireless website from a configuration-based shopping model into an **intent-based conversational experience**. The prototype will demonstrate how a chat-style interface can guide value customers through diagnosing their problem and arriving at a personalized plan or phone recommendation — without them having to browse or compare on their own.

---

## Problem Being Solved

The current experience is transactional and configuration-driven: users select a plan, choose a color, pick a device. This puts the burden of knowledge on the customer. Value customers — who are price-sensitive and utilitarian — don't want to browse; they want their specific problem solved quickly and cheaply.

---

## Target Audience

- **Prepay / value-tier customers** (not postpay Verizon customers)
- Price-sensitive: prioritizing ~$30–$40/month over premium features
- Not brand-loyal — they're shopping for utility, not status
- Motivated by solving an immediate, specific problem (e.g., running out of data mid-month, slow speeds, poor camera)

---

## Core Design Direction

### 1. Intent-First Entry — Quick Action Pills
Replace traditional navigation with a set of **conversational prompt chips** that surface the most common user pain points. Examples:
- "My data runs out too fast"
- "My phone is too slow"
- "I want better photos"
- "I'm running out of storage"
- "My plan costs too much"

These are framed around **what users want to achieve**, not what they want to buy.

### 2. Guided Conversational Flow
Once a user selects an intent, the experience shifts into a back-and-forth dialogue:
- Ask clarifying questions to understand the severity and context of the problem
- Keep questions short, visual, and easy to answer (buttons, not text inputs where possible)
- Build toward a recommendation — never make the user feel like they're in a sales funnel

### 3. Visual Recommendation Cards
When the conversation reaches a conclusion, the recommendation should be displayed as a **rich visual card** — not a plain text response. This card should:
- Mirror the look and feel of actual product/plan listings on the site
- Clearly show the upgrade cost differential (e.g., "$5 more/month")
- Highlight what problem it solves (e.g., "+4GB data = no more mid-month cutoffs")
- Include a clear CTA: upgrade, learn more, or talk to a real person

### 4. Two Primary Recommendation Paths
- **Plan upgrade** — resolves issues with data, speed, or coverage
- **Phone upgrade** — resolves issues with camera quality, storage, or performance

A third path — **talk to a real person** — should always be available as an escape hatch.

---

## Visual & Brand Direction

- Pull colors, typography, and component styles from the existing Total Wireless website
- Branding does not need to be pixel-perfect for this cut — directionally consistent is sufficient
- The conversational UI should feel lightweight and mobile-first
- Reference: Vinod's "Ask Radiant Digital" mockup as a structural/tonal inspiration

---

## What This Prototype Is NOT

- Not a fully functional chatbot with live data
- Not a complete buying flow (checkout, payment, etc.)
- Not a pixel-perfect brand execution

---

## Success Criteria

The prototype successfully demonstrates the concept if a stakeholder can look at it and immediately understand:
1. How intent-based entry replaces browse/search
2. How the conversation narrows to a recommendation
3. How the recommendation visually resembles a real product card — not a wall of text
